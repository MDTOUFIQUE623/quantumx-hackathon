In plain English: This is like a mini election office in your browser: check ID → get a voter pass → cast a secret ballot → get a receipt → anyone can see ballots were recorded (without names) → election officials count them. We use future-proof locks so votes stay safe even against tomorrow’s quantum computers.

Demo flow (≈5 min)

  1. Quick crypto check — prove browser and server speak the same “lock language” (30 s)
    Like checking that your phone and the bank app use the same security chip standard.
  2. Register + vote — pick a sample voter below (2 min)
    Show ID, get a digital voter pass, pick a candidate, submit a sealed ballot.
  3. Verify receipt — paste your ballot reference number (30 s)
    Like tracking a parcel: “Yes, your ballot is on the public list.”
  4. Public bulletin board — see recorded ballots without names (30 s)
    A glass wall showing locked ballot boxes, not who dropped them in.
  5. Count votes — official decrypts and publishes totals (1 min)
    Only the election officer has the master key to open boxes and count.
  6. Attack lab — try cheats; watch them fail (1 min)
    Fake ID, double voting, forged ballot — all blocked.
  7. SDG 16 story — why this matters for fair elections (2 min)

Why post-quantum? (elevator pitch)

Old locks (RSA / ECDSA)Our locks (Kyber + Dilithium)
Future quantum computers May break today’s math locks Different math puzzle — still hard to break
For elections Recorded votes could be read years later Ballots stay sealed for the long term
Example: Imagine someone photocopying every sealed envelope today, hoping a future machine can steam them open. We use envelopes designed so that even a quantum super-computer is not expected to open them quickly.

Quick glossary (for Q&A)

Identity Authority (IA)
Checks you are on the voter list and issues your signed voter pass.
Election Authority (EA)
Only role that can open sealed ballots and publish the count.
Ballot ID
Your receipt number — proves your ballot reached the public board (not who you voted for).
Kyber / Dilithium
Standard “quantum-safe” lock and signature types (like NIST-approved deadbolts).
403 / 409
HTTP codes meaning “not allowed” — e.g. wrong area (403) or already voted (409).
